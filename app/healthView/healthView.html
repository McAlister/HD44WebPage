Affordable Healthcare

It is a fact that per-capita healthcare costs are rising precipitously in America as compared to other countries. To add insult to injury the money isn't even getting us better results - we have shorter life spans and generally poorer health than many other first world countries. In the spirit of never letting a crisis go to waste a wide range of people want to assure you that this is caused by something they already hated and can only be solved by doing something they wanted to do anyway.

When debugging a broken system the starting questions are always, "When did this start happening?" and "What changed at that time?" Nobody is asking those questions.

{{getChar('history')}} Historical Health Spending

How did we get here?

Since 1970 inflation is responsible for roughly quintupling prices. We averaged $355 per person per year in health care expenditures back then. In 2016 we spent $10,348 per capita. That's about six times what inflation could explain. We've gained 4 years of life expectancy for our money, but others countries started ahead of us and have gained more in the same period for vastly less money. Our current system of multi-payer private insurance has been around since roughly the 1920's.

Everyone likes comparing our costs to Universal Health Care(UHC) countries today, but 40 years ago we were not so different from them. What changed? New technologies are increasing costs for everyone, but other places have the same technology we do without the high price tags.

It would be a very strong argument for UHC if what changed was that the other countries switched to UHC right around that time. Unfortunately the UK founded its NHS in 1948. Canada's system was passed in 1968 though prior systems were similar. Sweden's model of local responsibility dates back to the middle ages with formal regulation of the local hospital boards passing in 1864. Meanwhile the Swiss had very capitalist multi-payer till the mid-90's when they passed reforms that our own ACA is largely based on. Now we have basically the same system as them but we pay twice as much and die about 5 years sooner. So what we have is lots of countries all tackling this problem in very different ways whose common theme is they are now getting better results for much less money than we are. But they weren't in the '70s. So it is possible to do much better than we are now without fundamentally altering our system. This means that your local state representatives can make a meaningful difference even if they can't pass Single Payer.

When the taxpayer is on the hook for increasing health costs the government has a stake in monitoring costs, identifying when there are specific increases, investigating, and seeking to remedy them if possible. A UHC nation will always do these things in the name of reducing government waste and spending tax revenue wisely. The bulk of their savings isn't due to being Single Payer - its because of all the other things they do to save money. Things we could do if we wanted to.

We don't have to sit around waiting for Single Payer to start fixing things.

{{getChar('dtc')}} Eliminate Direct To Consumer Drug Advertising

Estimated Savings: $150 - $200 Billion

Only two countries in the world permit pharmaceutical companies to bypass doctors and advertise directly to patients, America and New Zealand. America lifted the ban on DTC drug ads in 1985. Shortly afterward drug expenditures in the United States started rapidly increasing from $12B a year in 1980 to $340B in 2016. In 1980 prescription drugs accounted for about 2.7% of total health spend. Today it is over 10% making pharmaceuticals a driving force behind our high costs. The American Medical Association advocates for a ban on DTC advertising.

Direct to consumer drug advertising increases health spend in many ways :

  • It convinces consumers that they need drugs they don't actually need. We know that doctors are susceptible to this pressure both from studies done on it and because of the difficulty in getting them to stop prescribing antibiotics for viral diseases even though this is creating super-bugs. Pushing back on patient demand is hard.
  • It creates a preference in patients for the most expensive drug available to treat a condition because that is the one they saw the ad for.
  • Every drug has a risk of an Adverse Drug Reaction(ADR). ADRs cost over $170 billion and kill over 100 thousand Americans every year. Unnecessary prescriptions contribute to that total with hospitalizations and ER visits. This study from 2013 estimated that between 5%-10% of hospital admissions and rising in the US are due to ADRs.
  • It encourages the scheduling of costly office visits for the sole purpose of getting or renewing an unnecessary prescription. Sometimes several if a patient is willing to go to different doctors in the search of someone who will prescribe what they want.
  • A frequent side effect of prescription drug use is weight gain which also increases health costs.

A particularly deadly part of this is the opioid epidemic which killed 442 Coloradans in 2016. 9% of prescriptions filled in Colorado in 2016 were for opioids.

{{getChar('fat')}} The Obesity Epidemic

Estimated Savings: $170 - $200 Billion

Another thing that started growing rapidly in 1980 was America's waistline. Today we are one of the fattest nations in the world and over 70% of Americans are overweight or obese. This has substantial cost impacts. Experts estimate that the financial burden of obesity is at least $1,429 per individual per year in 2008 dollars. Adjusted for inflation and increased obesity that's roughly $170B dollars per year. When you include costs associated with being merely overweight (over $200 per person per year) you exceed $200B.

On the upside, Colorado is the thinnest state in the nation with "only" 21% of adults and 15% of children being obese. This helps explain why Colorado has one of the lowest per-capita annual health costs in the nation. But that number has been steadily ticking upward over time and we need to reverse that trend.

{{getChar('test')}} Unnecessary Testing

Estimated Savings: $150 - $200 Billion

97% of doctors admit to knowingly ordering unnecessary tests and studies suggest that we spend over $200 Billion on them every year. Unnecessary testing isn't just a waste of money, it can also be harmful to patients. A CT scan exposes you to as much radiation as 400 chest X-rays. This is unhealthy and increases your future risk of cancer— particularly in children—so it should not be done without need.

The most expensive form of unnecessary testing is medical imaging. The CT scan was invented in 1972, while the MRI was invented in 1977. Both had to complete clinical trials before they could be commercialized which puts them in play in the 1980s. Those two tests alone are estimated as wasting $30 billion annually.

{{getChar('tort')}} Tort Reform

Estimated Savings: $0-$10 Billion

If we passed a law outlawing all malpractice suits tomorrow we would save less than $10 billion dollars. That is about three tenths of a percent of our annual health spend. So for every dollar you pay in health care costs you'd save, on average, a third of a penny. There simply isn't that much money being spent on it for it to be a meaningful contributor to the problem.

States that have passed even very draconian tort reforms are not seeing a bend in the cost curve. Can you tell when Texas passed tort reform from this graph? It was September 1st, 2003. And it did lower the number of suits and the payouts for victims of malpractice substantially. That is just such a small part of the system's costs that it simply doesn't matter to anyone ... except the victims of malpractice. It matters to them a great deal.

The only possible way that tort reform would matter was if it reduced unnecessary testing. We spent 20 times as much money on that as we do on medical lawsuits. If doctors really were ordering test just in case they needed them to defend themselves in court then they would stop once we passed tort reform. But they aren't. They never were. To quote general surgeon, Atul Gawande "I am far more concerned about doing too little than doing too much. It’s the scan, the test, the operation that I should have done that sticks with me—sometimes for years.". They aren't afraid of being sued. They are afraid of making a mistake that someone else has to suffer or die for.

Tort reform also doesn't address the category of tests that are unnecessary because they are duplicative - another doctor already ordered that test but the results are not available to the current doctor. Nor would it help situations where doctors are given financial incentives to order more tests. In some cases "fear of a malpractice suit" is used to justify behavior that is actually driven by profit motive. Three years after Texas passed tort reform a study found that one of it's cities had the highest per-capita Medicare costs in the nation. Investigation of the town found that when doctors also own the labs that do the tests ... well ... they order a lot more tests. And it probably isn't even conscious. It's just a conflict of interest that subtly influences their decision making in situations that aren't clear cut.
